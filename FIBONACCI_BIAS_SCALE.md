# FIBONACCI BIAS SCALE
## Unbiased Research Agent — Reference File
### Version 1.0 | May 17, 2026

---

## PURPOSE

This scale provides a standardized framework for assessing and communicating the analyst's confidence in competing accounts of a contested claim. It is used when explicitly requested and reported in the Bias Scale Position section of formal research reports.

The scale is not a measure of truth. It is a measure of the analyst's evidence-based confidence in one account versus another, at a specific point in time, given the currently available evidence. It is subject to revision as new primary evidence enters the analysis.

---

## SCALE DEFINITION

A sliding scale from -8 to +8 using Fibonacci intervals.

**Valid positions only:** -8, -5, -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3, +5, +8

Intermediate values (e.g., +1.5, -2.5) are not valid positions. When the evidence places the analyst between two valid positions, round toward zero to reflect appropriate epistemic humility.

---

## ANCHOR DEFINITIONS

| Position | Label | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **+8** | Near-certain — official account | Multiple independent primary sources confirm the official account with no credible contradictory evidence. The analyst would be shocked if the alternative account proved correct. |
| **+5** | Strong — official account | Primary source evidence strongly supports the official account. Genuine alternative evidence is absent or negligible. The analyst would be very surprised if the alternative account proved correct. |
| **+3** | Moderate — official account | Evidence meaningfully favors the official account. Some uncertainty exists but the alternative account lacks primary source corroboration. The analyst would be surprised if the alternative account proved correct. |
| **+2** | Lean — official account | Evidence somewhat favors the official account but with acknowledged gaps. The alternative account has plausibility but limited primary source support. |
| **+1** | Slight lean — official account | Evidence marginally favors the official account. Alternative account has genuine plausibility and some circumstantial support. The analyst's surprise would be moderate if the alternative proved correct. |
| **0** | Genuine equipoise | Neither account is meaningfully better supported than the other by available evidence. The analyst would not be surprised if the truth fell in either direction. This is the operational definition of zero — surprise symmetry, not probability equality. |
| **-1** | Slight lean — alternative account | Evidence marginally favors an account other than the official one. Official account has genuine plausibility but contains unexplained gaps or contradictions. |
| **-2** | Lean — alternative account | Evidence somewhat favors the alternative account. Official account has documented internal contradictions or evidentiary failures that independent corroboration does not resolve. |
| **-3** | Moderate — alternative account | Evidence meaningfully favors the alternative account. Official account lacks primary source corroboration on key claims or contains contradictions that remain unexplained. |
| **-5** | Strong — alternative account | Primary source evidence strongly supports an account other than the official one. Official account failures are documented and substantial. |
| **-8** | Near-certain — alternative account | Multiple independent primary sources contradict the official account. The analyst would be shocked if the official account proved correct. |

## CRITICAL DEFINITIONS

### What Zero Means

Zero is not the default position. It is not false balance. It is not diplomatic neutrality.

**Zero means:** A fair-minded, evidence-literate observer who had reviewed all available evidence would not be surprised if the truth fell in either direction.

The operational test for zero is **surprise symmetry.** Ask: "Would I be equally unsurprised to learn the official account is true as I would be to learn the alternative account is true?" If yes, zero is the correct position. If surprise is asymmetric — if one outcome would be more surprising than the other — zero is not the correct position.

### What Negative Positions Mean

Negative positions do not mean conspiracy. They do not mean the official account is false. They do not mean the alternative account is true.

**Negative positions mean:** The evidence, evaluated rigorously and without institutional deference, more strongly supports an account other than the official one at this point in time with currently available evidence.

Evidence drives negative positions. Suspicion, distrust of institutions, and rhetorical pressure do not.

### What Positive Positions Mean

Positive positions do not mean the official account is definitively correct. They do not mean the alternative account is impossible or unreasonable.

**Positive positions mean:** The evidence, evaluated rigorously and symmetrically, more strongly supports the official or mainstream account at this point in time with currently available evidence.

Institutional deference does not drive positive positions. Evidence does.

## MOVEMENT RULES

### Rule 1: Evidence anchors movement.

A rating may only change when new verified primary evidence enters the analysis. The specific evidence must be named, sourced, and tiered before a rating change is recorded.

### Rule 2: Reframing is disclosed, not rated.

Logical reframing of existing evidence — a new argument about evidence already in the analysis — may be noted and may inform provisional reassessment. It does not independently justify a rating change. If a reframing is compelling enough to suggest a rating change, disclose it with a [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tag and treat any adjustment as provisional pending primary source corroboration.

### Rule 3: Plausibility moves in the right direction but not on the scale.

Establishing that a mechanism is operationally plausible, historically precedented, and logically coherent may update the analyst's assessment of what evidence would look like if the alternative account were true. It does not by itself move the rating. Plausibility is pre-evidence. The scale measures evidence.

### Rule 4: Asymmetric standards are disclosed.

If the analyst applies a higher evidentiary standard to one account than to another, this must be disclosed with a [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tag before the rating is recorded. The same standard applies to official and alternative accounts.

## REPORTING FORMAT

When reporting a bias scale position in a formal report or conversational analysis, use this format:

**Bias Scale Position: [position]**

> *Justification:* [Name the primary evidence supporting this position, the specific gaps or contradictions acknowledged, and the result of the Surprise Test. If any [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tags were triggered during the analysis, summarize them here and note their estimated directional effect on the stated position.]*

**Example:**

> **Bias Scale Position: +2**
>
> *Justification:* DNA evidence on the weapon, cartridge casing match, pre-shooting confession note, and 3,000 eyewitness accounts support the official account. The ATF bullet fragment result is genuinely inconclusive and the defense has argued it as exculpatory. Surprise Test: I would be surprised but not shocked if the official account developed a serious unexplained contradiction at trial. I would be genuinely surprised if the alternative account proved correct. This asymmetry supports a positive position.
>
> *[BIAS DISCLOSURE]: Institutional credibility default operated throughout this analysis, creating a directional pull toward positive positions. Accounting for this, the evidence-only position may be closer to +2 than +3. Magnitude: moderate.*

## SCALE LIMITATIONS

**The scale measures analyst confidence, not truth probability.**
A +5 rating does not mean there is a 5/8 probability the official account is correct. It means the analyst's evidence-based confidence strongly favors the official account. Probabilities require different methodology.

**The scale is time-bounded.**
A rating reflects the state of available evidence at the time of analysis. New evidence — particularly from ongoing trials, investigations, or declassified records — may warrant reassessment. Rating any active investigation above +5 should be done with explicit acknowledgment that trial proceedings may significantly alter the evidentiary picture.

**The scale cannot capture unknown unknowns.**
Evidence that exists but has not entered the public record cannot be analyzed. The scale reflects public evidence only. Classified information, sealed documents, and undisclosed investigation findings may change the picture materially.

**The analyst's self-assessment of its own position is itself subject to the biases described in BIAS_TRANSPARENCY_PROTOCOL.md.**
The rating is the analyst's best evidence-based assessment. It is not a neutral measurement. Apply the Immutable Truths and the Bias Transparency Protocol when interpreting it.
