# FIBONACCI POLITICAL BIAS SCALE
## Unbiased Research Agent — Reference File
### Version 1.0 | May 2026

---

## PURPOSE

This scale provides a standardized framework for assessing and communicating the political or ideological lean of a source, a claim, or a piece of analysis. It is applied by the analyst during research and reported in the Source Evaluation and Contested Claims sections of formal research reports.

The scale measures one thing: how politically or ideologically slanted is this content, and in which direction?

It does not measure factual accuracy. It does not measure evidentiary weight between competing accounts. Those are separate instruments. This scale measures political and ideological bias alone.

**Critical distinction:** This scale is applied twice for every assessment — once to the source producing the information, and once to the content of the claim itself. A left-leaning source can report an unbiased claim. A centrist outlet can publish a heavily slanted opinion piece. Source bias and content bias must always be assessed and reported separately.

---

## SCALE DEFINITION

A sliding scale from -8 to +8 using Fibonacci intervals.

**Valid positions only:** -8, -5, -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3, +5, +8

**Negative positions** = left-leaning political or ideological bias
**Zero** = no detectable political or ideological bias
**Positive positions** = right-leaning political or ideological bias

Intermediate values (e.g., +1.5, -2.5) are not valid positions. When the evidence places the analyst between two valid positions, round toward zero to reflect appropriate epistemic humility.

## CRITICAL DEFINITIONS

### What Zero Means

Zero is not the diplomatic default. It is not what you report when you cannot detect a lean. It is not false balance between two sides.

**Zero means:** After rigorous examination of sourcing choices, language, framing, evidence selection, and omissions, no consistent political or ideological lean is detectable in either direction.

Zero is an evidence-based finding. Many sources and many claims are genuinely unbiased on specific topics even if they carry a lean on others. Source bias ratings should be topic-specific where possible — a source that leans right on immigration coverage may be centrist on financial reporting.

### What the Fibonacci Intervals Mean

The jumps between valid positions are intentional and consequential. Moving from -3 to -5 is not a small step. It requires substantially stronger evidence of ideological commitment than moving from -2 to -3. The non-linear intervals force the analyst to justify large movements rather than drifting incrementally toward the extremes.

**The key thresholds:**
- **±1 to ±2:** Detectable lean, minor significance
- **±2 to ±3:** Meaningful lean, worth noting in source evaluation
- **±3 to ±5:** Strong lean, independent corroboration required before treating as evidence
- **±5 to ±8:** Extreme lean, treat as advocacy rather than reporting

### The Dual Assessment Requirement

Every political bias assessment produces two ratings, not one:

1. **Source bias rating** — the political lean of the outlet, organization, or individual producing the information, based on their documented pattern of coverage across topics and time
2. **Content bias rating** — the political lean of this specific claim, article, or piece of analysis, based on its internal construction, framing choices, evidence selection, and omissions

These must be reported separately. Never collapse them into a single rating.

## MOVEMENT RULES

### Rule 1: Evidence anchors movement.

A rating may only change when new verified evidence of ideological lean enters the analysis — documented editorial patterns, funding disclosures, language analysis, or sourcing choices. The specific evidence must be named and sourced before a rating change is recorded.

### Rule 2: Reframing is disclosed, not rated.

A new argument about existing evidence of bias may be noted and may inform provisional reassessment. It does not independently justify a rating change. Disclose with a [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tag and treat any adjustment as provisional.

### Rule 3: Political disagreement is not the same as political bias.

A claim that challenges mainstream consensus, criticizes a government, or aligns with one political party's position is not automatically biased. Bias is a property of how information is constructed and framed — not of what conclusions it reaches. A claim can reach an uncomfortable or politically inconvenient conclusion and still be unbiased if the evidence is presented fairly and completely.

### Rule 4: Asymmetric standards are disclosed.

The same standard for detecting bias must be applied to left-leaning and right-leaning content. If the analyst finds it easier to identify bias in one direction than the other, this must be disclosed with a [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tag. The Bias Transparency Protocol applies here.

## REPORTING FORMAT

When reporting political bias ratings in a formal report or conversational analysis, use this format:

**Political Bias Rating — Source: [position]**
**Political Bias Rating — Content: [position]**

> *Justification:* [Name the specific evidence of bias — language choices, sourcing patterns, omissions, framing decisions — that support this position. Note any [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tags triggered during the assessment.]*

**Example:**

> **Political Bias Rating — Source: -3**
> **Political Bias Rating — Content: -1**
>
> *Justification:* The outlet has a documented center-left editorial orientation per independent media bias assessors, with consistent framing choices that favor progressive interpretations on social policy topics. This specific article, however, presents the core claim with minimal ideological framing and includes counter-evidence from right-of-center sources. The content bias is therefore rated lower than the source bias.
>
> *[BIAS DISCLOSURE]: Mainstream consensus anchoring — my source bias rating draws on third-party media bias assessments rather than independent primary analysis of the outlet's coverage patterns. Those assessments themselves may carry methodological bias. Magnitude: minor.*

## SCALE LIMITATIONS

**The scale measures detectable bias, not intent.**
A source or claim can be politically biased without conscious intent. Bias embedded in sourcing choices, framing, or omissions may reflect institutional culture rather than deliberate advocacy. The scale rates the output, not the motivation.

**The scale is topic-specific.**
A source rated -3 on immigration coverage may be 0 on financial reporting. Where possible, source bias ratings should specify the topic domain rather than treating them as global assessments.

**The scale is time-bounded.**
Editorial orientations change. A rating reflects the source's documented pattern at the time of analysis and should be revisited for sources whose ownership, leadership, or editorial direction has recently changed.

**Bias and accuracy are independent dimensions.**
A strongly biased source can report accurate facts. An unbiased source can report inaccurate claims. Never use a political bias rating as a proxy for accuracy. Consult the Fibonacci Accuracy Scale for factual accuracy assessment. The two scales must be applied and reported separately.

**The analyst's own political orientation is a bias risk.**
Detecting political bias in others requires the analyst to have a calibrated sense of center. The analyst's own trained dispositions may make certain forms of bias more or less visible. Apply the Bias Transparency Protocol — particularly the mainstream consensus anchoring and harm-avoidance tension disclosures — when assessing political bias.
