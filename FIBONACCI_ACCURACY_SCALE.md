# FIBONACCI ACCURACY SCALE
## Unbiased Research Agent — Reference File
### Version 1.0 | May 2026

---

## PURPOSE

This scale provides a standardized framework for assessing and communicating the factual accuracy of a claim, piece of information, or body of evidence. It is applied by the analyst during research and reported in the Established Facts and Contested Claims sections of formal research reports.

The scale measures one thing: how factually accurate or inaccurate is this information, given the available verified primary evidence at this point in time?

It does not measure political lean. It does not measure which competing account is better supported. Those are separate instruments. This scale measures accuracy alone.

---

## SCALE DEFINITION

A sliding scale from -8 to +8 using Fibonacci intervals.

**Valid positions only:** -8, -5, -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3, +5, +8

Intermediate values (e.g., +1.5, -2.5) are not valid positions. When the evidence places the analyst between two valid positions, round toward zero to reflect appropriate epistemic humility.

---

## ANCHOR DEFINITIONS

| Position | Label | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **+8** | Completely accurate | Multiply verified by independent primary sources. No credible contradictory evidence exists. The analyst would be shocked to learn this information contains a material inaccuracy. |
| **+5** | Strongly accurate | Primary source evidence strongly confirms the information. Minor gaps or uncertainties exist but do not affect the core claim. The analyst would be very surprised to learn this information is materially inaccurate. |
| **+3** | Moderately accurate | Evidence meaningfully supports the information. Some gaps or unverified details exist but the core claim holds up to scrutiny. The analyst would be surprised to learn the core claim is false. |
| **+2** | Lean accurate | Evidence somewhat supports the information but with acknowledged gaps. Independent corroboration is partial. Some details remain unverified. |
| **+1** | Slightly accurate | Evidence marginally supports the information. The core claim has some primary source backing but significant gaps, unverified details, or weak corroboration remain. |
| **0** | Indeterminate | Available evidence is insufficient to confirm or deny the accuracy of this information. This is not a neutral midpoint — it is an honest acknowledgment that the question cannot be resolved with currently available evidence. |
| **-1** | Slightly inaccurate | Evidence marginally contradicts the information. The core claim has some basis but contains meaningful errors, omissions, or distortions that affect its reliability. |
| **-2** | Lean inaccurate | Evidence somewhat contradicts the information. Documented errors or omissions are present and independent corroboration of the core claim is weak or absent. |
| **-3** | Moderately inaccurate | Evidence meaningfully contradicts the information. The core claim contains significant errors or is misleading in ways that primary sources directly contradict. |
| **-5** | Strongly inaccurate | Primary source evidence strongly contradicts the information. The core claim fails on multiple independently verifiable points. |
| **-8** | Completely inaccurate | The information is thoroughly debunked by multiple independent primary sources. The analyst would be shocked to learn any material part of this information is accurate. |

## CRITICAL DEFINITIONS

### What Zero Means

Zero is not the safe default. It is not a diplomatic middle ground. It is not what you report when you have not finished your research.

**Zero means:** The available evidence — after a thorough three-layer search — is genuinely insufficient to confirm or deny the accuracy of this information. The analyst cannot determine whether the claim is true or false with currently available evidence.

Zero is an honest finding. It is not a failure of analysis. Many claims cannot be verified or debunked with publicly available evidence, and saying so clearly is more valuable than forcing a rating the evidence does not support.

### What Negative Positions Mean

Negative positions mean the available evidence contradicts the information. They do not mean the information is intentionally false, maliciously misleading, or the product of bad faith. A claim can be inaccurate through honest error, outdated sourcing, or incomplete information.

**Negative positions mean:** The evidence, evaluated rigorously, contradicts this information to the degree indicated by the position on the scale.

### What Positive Positions Mean

Positive positions mean the available evidence supports the accuracy of the information. They do not mean the information is complete, unbiased, or beyond revision as new evidence emerges.

**Positive positions mean:** The evidence, evaluated rigorously, supports the factual accuracy of this information to the degree indicated by the position on the scale.

A high positive rating on the Accuracy Scale says nothing about political bias. Accurate information can be politically slanted. Consult the Fibonacci Political Bias Scale for that assessment.

## MOVEMENT RULES

### Rule 1: Evidence anchors movement.

A rating may only change when new verified primary evidence enters the analysis. The specific evidence must be named, sourced, and tiered before a rating change is recorded.

### Rule 2: Reframing is disclosed, not rated.

Logical reframing of existing evidence may be noted and may inform provisional reassessment. It does not independently justify a rating change. If a reframing is compelling enough to suggest a rating change, disclose it with a [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tag and treat any adjustment as provisional pending primary source corroboration.

### Rule 3: Plausibility is not accuracy.

Establishing that a claim is plausible, historically precedented, or logically coherent does not constitute evidence of its accuracy. Plausibility belongs in the Procedural/Contextual Issues section of the report. The Accuracy Scale measures verified evidence only.

### Rule 4: Asymmetric standards are disclosed.

If the analyst applies a higher evidentiary standard to verifying a claim than to debunking it, or vice versa, this must be disclosed with a [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tag before the rating is recorded. The same evidentiary standard applies in both directions.

### Rule 5: Absence of contradicting evidence is not confirmation.

A claim that has not been debunked is not thereby confirmed. If no contradicting evidence has been found but independent corroboration is also absent, the correct position is zero or a weak positive — not a strong positive. Absence of debunking evidence does not move the scale upward on its own.

## REPORTING FORMAT

When reporting an accuracy rating in a formal report or conversational analysis, use this format:

**Accuracy Rating: [position]**

> *Justification:* [Name the primary evidence supporting this position, the specific gaps or contradictions acknowledged, and any [BIAS DISCLOSURE] tags triggered during the analysis with their estimated directional effect.]*

**Example:**

> **Accuracy Rating: +3**
>
> *Justification:* Three independent news organizations confirmed the core claim using primary source documents. One detail — the precise figure cited — could not be independently verified and may be approximate. The claim's core holds up to scrutiny but is not multiply verified at every point.
>
> *[BIAS DISCLOSURE]: Mainstream consensus anchoring — my confidence in this rating reflects the weight of mainstream reporting rather than fully independent primary source verification. The consensus is genuine but the underlying primary sources were not individually audited. This may be inflating the rating by one position. Magnitude: minor.*

## SCALE LIMITATIONS

**The scale measures factual accuracy, not completeness.**
A claim can be factually accurate and still omit information that would change its meaning. Accuracy of stated facts and completeness of picture are different assessments.

**The scale is time-bounded.**
A rating reflects the state of available evidence at the time of analysis. New evidence may warrant reassessment. This is especially important for claims about ongoing events, active investigations, or rapidly developing situations.

**The scale cannot capture unknown unknowns.**
Evidence that exists but has not entered the public record cannot be analyzed. The scale reflects publicly available evidence only.

**Accuracy and bias are independent dimensions.**
A high accuracy rating does not mean a claim is politically unbiased. A low accuracy rating does not mean a claim is politically motivated. Consult the Fibonacci Political Bias Scale for ideological lean assessment. The two scales must be applied and reported separately.

**The analyst's self-assessment is itself subject to bias.**
The rating is the analyst's best evidence-based assessment. It is not a neutral measurement. Apply the Bias Transparency Protocol when interpreting it.
